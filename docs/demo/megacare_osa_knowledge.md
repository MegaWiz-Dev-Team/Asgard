## 1. Overview

Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA) is a common chronic disorder characterized by repeated episodes of complete (apnea) or partial (hypopnea) upper airway obstruction during sleep. These events lead to intermittent hypoxemia, hypercapnia, and sleep fragmentation. OSA affects approximately 10-30% of adults worldwide, with prevalence increasing with age and obesity.

### Key Facts
- **Prevalence**: 10-30% of adults; up to 50% in certain high-risk populations
- **Male:Female ratio**: 2:1 to 3:1 (gap narrows after menopause)
- **Undiagnosed rate**: Estimated 80% of moderate-to-severe cases remain undiagnosed
- **Economic impact**: $150 billion/year in the US alone (accidents, lost productivity, healthcare costs)

---

## 2. Pathophysiology

OSA occurs when the muscles in the back of the throat relax excessively during sleep, causing the airway to narrow or close. This leads to:

1. **Apnea**: Complete cessation of airflow for ≥10 seconds
2. **Hypopnea**: ≥30% reduction in airflow with ≥3% oxygen desaturation or arousal
3. **Respiratory Effort-Related Arousal (RERA)**: Increasing respiratory effort leading to arousal without meeting apnea/hypopnea criteria

### Contributing Factors
- **Anatomical**: Large tongue, retrognathia, tonsillar hypertrophy, deviated septum
- **Neuromuscular**: Reduced pharyngeal dilator muscle tone during sleep
- **Obesity**: Fat deposition around the upper airway (BMI >30 significantly increases risk)
- **Age**: Loss of muscle tone and tissue elasticity
- **Genetics**: Craniofacial structure, body fat distribution

---

## 3. Classification by Severity (AHI)

The **Apnea-Hypopnea Index (AHI)** is the primary metric for classifying OSA severity:

| Severity | AHI (events/hour) | Clinical Significance |
|----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Normal | < 5 | No clinically significant OSA |
| Mild | 5 - 14 | Snoring, mild daytime sleepiness |
| Moderate | 15 - 30 | Significant daytime sleepiness, increased cardiovascular risk |
| Severe | > 30 | High cardiovascular risk, cognitive impairment, accidents |

### Additional Metrics
- **Oxygen Desaturation Index (ODI)**: Number of ≥3% oxygen desaturation events per hour
- **Lowest SpO₂ (nadir)**: Minimum oxygen saturation during sleep
  - Normal: >90%
  - Mild desaturation: 85-89%
  - Moderate desaturation: 80-84%
  - Severe desaturation: <80%
- **Arousal Index**: Number of arousals per hour of sleep

## 4. Clinical Presentation

### Primary Symptoms
- **Nocturnal**: Loud snoring, witnessed apneas, choking/gasping, nocturia, dry mouth
- **Diurnal**: Excessive daytime sleepiness (EDS), morning headaches, cognitive impairment, mood disturbances, decreased libido

### Screening Tools
- **STOP-BANG Questionnaire** (score ≥3 = high risk):
  - **S**noring loudly
  - **T**ired/sleepy during the day
  - **O**bserved apneas
  - **P**ressure (blood pressure) - hypertension
  - **B**MI > 35
  - **A**ge > 50
  - **N**eck circumference > 40 cm
  - **G**ender - male
- **Epworth Sleepiness Scale (ESS)**: Score >10 suggests excessive sleepiness
- **Berlin Questionnaire**: Risk categories based on snoring, sleepiness, and BMI/hypertension

## 5. Diagnosis

### Gold Standard: Polysomnography (PSG)
- Attended, in-laboratory overnight sleep study
- Measures: EEG, EOG, EMG, ECG, airflow, respiratory effort, SpO₂, body position, limb movements
- Required for: Complex cases, suspected comorbid sleep disorders, pediatric patients

### Home Sleep Apnea Test (HSAT)
- For uncomplicated adult patients with high pre-test probability of moderate-to-severe OSA
- Measures: Airflow, respiratory effort, SpO₂ (minimum Type III device)
- **Not suitable for**: Central sleep apnea, significant comorbidities, pediatric patients

### Diagnostic Criteria (AASM/ICSD-3)
OSA is diagnosed when:
- AHI ≥ 5 events/hour WITH associated symptoms (sleepiness, witnessed apneas, etc.)
- OR AHI ≥ 15 events/hour regardless of symptoms

## 6. Treatment Options

### 6.1 Continuous Positive Airway Pressure (CPAP)
- **Gold standard treatment** for moderate-to-severe OSA
- Delivers continuous positive pressure via nasal or full-face mask
- Effective in reducing AHI to <5 events/hour in most patients
- **Adherence**: ≥4 hours/night for ≥70% of nights is considered adequate
- **Pressure range**: Typically 4-20 cmH₂O
- **Auto-PAP (APAP)**: Adjusts pressure automatically (4-20 cmH₂O range)

### 6.2 Oral Appliances (OA)
- Mandibular Advancement Devices (MAD)
- For mild-to-moderate OSA or CPAP-intolerant patients
- Custom-fitted by qualified dentist
- Less effective than CPAP but better adherence

### 6.3 Positional Therapy
- For positional OSA (AHI significantly worse in supine position)
- Devices to prevent supine sleeping
- Most effective as adjunctive therapy

### 6.4 Surgical Options
- **Uvulopalatopharyngoplasty (UPPP)**: Tissue removal from soft palate
- **Maxillomandibular Advancement (MMA)**: Jaw repositioning (most effective surgery, 86% success)
- **Hypoglossal Nerve Stimulation (Inspire)**: Implantable device for moderate-to-severe OSA
- **Tonsillectomy/Adenoidectomy**: First-line for pediatric OSA
- **Bariatric Surgery**: For morbidly obese patients with OSA

### 6.5 Lifestyle Modifications
- Weight loss (10% weight loss can reduce AHI by 26-50%)
- Alcohol avoidance (especially 3-4 hours before bed)
- Smoking cessation
- Sleep hygiene optimization
- Exercise (even without weight loss, reduces AHI by ~25%)

## 7. Complications & Comorbidities

### Cardiovascular
- Hypertension (50% of OSA patients)
- Atrial fibrillation (2-4x increased risk)
- Heart failure
- Stroke (2-3x increased risk)
- Coronary artery disease

### Metabolic
- Type 2 diabetes (independent risk factor)
- Metabolic syndrome
- Dyslipidemia

### Neurological
- Cognitive impairment
- Depression and anxiety
- Increased motor vehicle accident risk (2-7x)

### Other
- Perioperative complications
- Pregnancy complications (gestational hypertension, preeclampsia)
- Nocturia

## 9. Special Populations

### Pediatric OSA
- Prevalence: 1-5%
- Primary cause: Adenotonsillar hypertrophy
- First-line treatment: Adenotonsillectomy
- AHI thresholds differ: >1 event/hour is abnormal in children

### OSA in Pregnancy
- Increased risk in 3rd trimester
- Associated with gestational hypertension, preeclampsia, gestational diabetes
- CPAP is safe during pregnancy

### Elderly Patients
- Higher prevalence but clinical significance may differ
- Treatment decisions should consider symptoms and comorbidities
- Lower CPAP pressures often sufficient
